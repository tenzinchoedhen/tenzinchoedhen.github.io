From Code to Insights: My Journey into Business Analytics


If someone told me a few years ago that I'd be knee-deep in data analytics, working towards getting a Master's degree in Business Analytics, I probably would have laughed and gone back to debugging code. But here we are.

Before diving into the details of my journey, let me set the stage with a little industry context—because believe it or not, tariffs can be the villain in this story. In the Japanese automobile manufacturing industry, every single part—from the smallest bolt to entire truck engines—needs a Certificate of Origin (COO) to prove it’s made in Japan. If these certificates aren’t handled properly, companies face heavy tariffs when exporting. Imagine building a flawless truck, only to have unnecessary costs piled on because someone mishandled paperwork. Not exactly the efficiency dream of a manufacturing giant.

When I joined Mitsubishi Fuso Truck and Bus Corporation’s Digital Transformation Office, I quickly realized that the process of managing these COOs was a mess of manual work, endless emails, and human error. It was like watching someone try to untangle a pair of wired earphones—frustrating and time-consuming.

That’s where I stepped in.


The COO Automation Project: Turning Tangled Processes into Streamlined Solutions

My background in software development meant I was used to breaking down complex problems and building solutions from the ground up. But applying that mindset in a business setting—where the stakes weren’t just crashing apps but real financial consequences—was both thrilling and challenging.

The project that really defined my time at Mitsubishi Fuso was automating the Certificate of Origin process. Using Microsoft's Power Automate workflows, I created an automated system that streamlined communication with suppliers and eliminated the need for tedious manual input. This wasn’t just a "nice to have" feature—it was transformative. The new system reduced 640 manual work hours annually and saved the company a whopping 3.2 million JPY every year. But beyond the numbers, it fundamentally reshaped how the company approached certificate delivery to end customers, reducing the risk of costly tariffs and ensuring smoother operations.


From Software Developer to Business Analyst: Lessons Learned

What stood out to me most during this project was how transferable my software development skills were to business analytics. Debugging code isn’t all that different from debugging a business process—both require a sharp eye for inefficiencies and a methodical approach to problem-solving. The key difference? The "bugs" in business processes often have a much bigger ripple effect.

I learned to see data not just as numbers or lines of code, but as powerful tools for storytelling and decision-making. Each automated workflow wasn’t just a piece of tech—it was a solution that directly impacted people’s daily work and the company’s bottom line. That’s when I realized I wanted to dive deeper into business intelligence, using data-driven insights to identify opportunities for digital transformation.


Looking Ahead

My time at Mitsubishi Fuso solidified my passion for combining technology and analytics to drive meaningful change. Now, as a graduate student in business analytics, I’m eager to build on this foundation and continue finding ways to automate processes, reduce costs, and help businesses make smarter decisions.

Because at the end of the day, whether it's untangling code or business processes, there's nothing more satisfying than finding a solution that just… works!

Stay tuned for more stories from my journey into the world of business intelligence—and maybe a few lessons learned along the way.
